Nuclear Reactions

Nucleus Stability

Stable nuclei have...

  • 83 or fewer protons.
  • Large unstable nuclei with more than 83 protons are radioactive.
  • As unstable nuclei decay (fission), they give off energy.

Coal burning generating plant

The heat from burning coal turns water into steam. Steam pressure turns the turbine.

Nuclear generating plant

The heat from fissioning uranium turns water into steam. Steam pressure turns the turbine.

Fission reactions

  • Uranium-235 nuclei can fission (split)
  • The reaction products are smaller fission fragments, fast moving neutrons and energy.
  • A chain reaction occurs when other U-235 nuclei absorb the neutrons. These U-235 nuclei fission and emit more neutrons.

Energy from fission

Energy is released due to the difference in binding energy between uranium nuclei and the smaller nuclei of fission fragments.

Moderator and control rods

When U-235 nuclei fission, they emit fast moving neutrons. Other U-235 nuclei absorb these neutrons more easily when the neutrons move slowly.

  • A moderator (usually water of graphite) slows the velocity of the fast-moving neutrons. Fuel rods are placed in the moderator.
  • Control rods control the rate of the reaction by absorbing neutrons. Control rods (usually cadmium or boron) can slow down or stop the reaction.

Fuel rods, coolant, containment systems

  1. Fuel rods or ceramic fuel pellets are filled with a mixture of two isotopes of uranium: U-235 and U-238.
  2. Coolant (usually water or helium gas) transfers heat from the reactor core to the vessel that produces steam to turn the turbins.
  3. Containment systems prevent radiation from escaping into the environment. Double containment systems are safer.
  4. The heat exchanger heats water into steam. Steam pressure turns the turbins that spin magnets near coils of wire to generate electricity.

Fuel for conventional nuclear reactors

Naturally-occuring uranium ore consists of uranium-238 and uranium-235

  • Ore is about 99.3% uranium-238 and only 0.7% uranium-235.
  • Fission reactors use U-235.
  • Ore must be enriched to decrease the percentage of U-238 and increase the percentage of U-235.

Chernobyl reactor accident

  • A power surge caused an uncontrolled fission chain reaction.
  • The water coolant molecules split into hydrogen and oxygen gas. The gas exploded.
  • Radioactive material was blown into the environment. The graphite moderator caught on fire.
  • Initially, it was feared another uncontrolled chain reaction could occur. It did not.
  • The reactor has been sealed by a massive arch. The debris inside will remain radioactive for thousands of years.

Fukushima nuclear accident

What happened? An earthquake triggered a tsunami wave that flooded the Fukushima nuclear power plant.

Result: A series of explosions and a meltdown of three reactor cores released radition into the air and the ocean.

Why did this happen?

  • The backup generators and cooling system controls in the basement of the building flooded.
  • The reactor cores overheated.
  • The intense heat split water into hydrogen and oxygen gas. The hydrogen gas exploded.

A safer reactor: Reactor cores are placed at the lowest level of the building. In an accident, water drains by gravity to cool the reactor and prevent a meltdown.
